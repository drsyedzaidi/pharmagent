Handling Missing Data in R

Phase 03: Classical Methods — Deletion & Single Imputation

Before multiple imputation existed, analysts handled missing data with two blunt tools:
throw the incomplete rows away, or fill the holes with a single "best guess." Both are
still the default behaviour of most statistical software, and both are still the most
common way missing data is handled in practice. This phase dissects them from first
principles so you understand exactly what each one costs. You will see — algebraically and
by simulation against a known ground truth — that listwise deletion is unbiased only
under MCAR and can be badly biased under MAR; that mean imputation shrinks variance,
attenuates correlations and slopes toward zero, and lies about standard errors; and that
even the smarter deterministic regression imputation still understates uncertainty because
every filled value sits exactly on the regression line. The fix — adding a random residual
draw — is the conceptual bridge to the multiple imputation of Phase 04. By the end you can
justify, not just execute, the decision to drop or to fill.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish complete-case (listwise) from available-case (pairwise) deletion, and state when each is valid, biased, or numerically unstable
  • Explain why "just drop the NAs" is a modelling decision — unbiased under MCAR, potentially biased under MAR/MNAR — not a harmless default
  • Derive and demonstrate that mean imputation shrinks the sample variance by a factor of $(n_{obs}-1)/(n-1)$ and attenuates correlations and regression slopes toward zero
  • Explain why single imputation understates standard errors: it treats imputed values as if they had been observed
  • Implement mean, median, and mode imputation, and stratified (within-subgroup) imputation, in base R
  • Contrast deterministic regression imputation (inflates correlations, still too little variance) with stochastic regression imputation (restores variance and covariance)
  • Recover — and compare against truth — the mean, variance, correlation, and slope under each classical method by simulation

Prerequisites

Phase 01 (the MCAR/MAR/MNAR taxonomy; the missingness indicator) and Phase 02
(descriptive diagnostics; the shadow matrix; why the data cannot confirm MNAR). Working R
with dplyr and ggplot2. Comfort with sample mean, variance, covariance, correlation,
and ordinary least-squares regression. Install:
install.packages(c("dplyr", "ggplot2", "tidyr", "mice")) (mice::mice(..., method = "norm.nob")
is used only to cross-check the hand-rolled stochastic regression imputer).
