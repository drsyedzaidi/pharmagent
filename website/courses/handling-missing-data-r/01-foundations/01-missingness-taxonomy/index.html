Lesson 01: Missing Data — Definition, Impact, and the MCAR/MAR/MNAR Taxonomy

Objective. Define missing data formally, explain why it threatens the validity of an analysis, and master the Rubin (1976) taxonomy — MCAR, MAR, MNAR — that governs which methods are legitimate.

Prerequisites. Basic statistics (expectation, bias, conditional probability). No prior missing-data theory required.

Concept (from first principles)

What "missing data" actually is

Start with a complete data matrix you wish you had:

$$Y = \{y_{ij}\}, \quad i = 1,\dots,n \text{ (units)}, \quad j = 1,\dots,p \text{ (variables)}.$$

Reality only lets you observe some of those cells. Encode which cells you got with a
missingness indicator matrix the same shape as $Y$:

$$R = \{r_{ij}\}, \qquad r_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & y_{ij} \text{ observed} \\ 0 & y_{ij} \text{ missing.} \end{cases}$$

Partition the intended data into what you actually see and what you don't:

$$Y = (Y_{\text{obs}}, Y_{\text{mis}}).$$

This is the entire setup. The deep insight of Rubin (1976) is that $R$ is itself a random
variable with its own distribution, and the whole game is understanding how $R$ relates
to $Y$. Missing data is not just "holes in a table" — it is a second stochastic process
sitting on top of your data-generating process. Ignore that second process and you can be
badly, invisibly wrong.

Missing ≠ zero, ≠ blank, ≠ "not applicable." A missing systolic blood pressure is
not a blood pressure of 0. A concentration below an assay's limit of quantification is
present but uncertain, not absent. Confusing these is the most common first-day error.

Why missing data is dangerous

Three separate harms, all real, often occurring together.

1. Bias. If the units you keep differ systematically from the units you lose, any
estimate computed on the survivors is an estimate for the wrong population. Suppose you
want $\mathbb{E}[Y]$ but heavier individuals are less likely to report weight. The mean of
the observed weights,

$$\hat\mu_{\text{obs}} = \frac{1}{n_{\text{obs}}}\sum_{i:\,r_i = 1} y_i,$$

targets $\mathbb{E}[Y \mid R = 1]$, not $\mathbb{E}[Y]$. These are equal only under special
conditions (below). Otherwise $\hat\mu_{\text{obs}}$ is biased no matter how large $n$ is —
more data does not fix it.

2. Reduced power / precision. Even when unbiased, throwing away rows shrinks the
effective sample size. Standard errors scale roughly as $1/\sqrt{n_{\text{obs}}}$, so
dropping 30% of rows inflates every confidence interval and weakens every test. In a
regression with several partially-observed covariates, listwise deletion can silently
discard the majority of rows even when no single variable is badly missing.

3. Invalid inference. The subtlest harm: a method can produce a point estimate and a
standard error that both look fine but are internally inconsistent. "Valid" is a
two-part promise — the estimate must be (approximately) unbiased and its reported
uncertainty must be correct. A single imputed value plugged in as if it were real
data satisfies neither: it biases nothing yet lies about how much you know, understating
the standard error because it pretends an imputed guess is an observation.

Rubin's taxonomy: MCAR, MAR, MNAR

The mechanism is defined by the conditional distribution of the missingness indicator $R$0
given the data. Let $R$1 denote unknown parameters of the missingness process.

MCAR — Missing Completely At Random. Missingness depends on neither the observed nor
the unobserved data:

$$P(R \mid Y_{\text{obs}}, Y_{\text{mis}}, \phi) = P(R \mid \phi).$$

The probability a cell is missing is a coin flip unrelated to anything in the study. The
observed cases are a genuine random subsample of the intended sample. This is the friendly
case: almost everything works, you just have less of it.

MAR — Missing At Random. Missingness depends only on the observed data:

$$P(R \mid Y_{\text{obs}}, Y_{\text{mis}}, \phi) = P(R \mid Y_{\text{obs}}, \phi).$$

Given what you have observed, the probability of missingness carries no further
information about the missing values themselves. The name is misleading — MAR is not
"random." It means the missingness is fully explainable by variables you observed. This is
the workhorse assumption: methods that use the correlation between observed and missing
variables (multiple imputation, maximum likelihood) recover unbiased estimates under MAR.

MNAR — Missing Not At Random. Missingness depends on the unobserved value itself
(possibly after conditioning on the observed data):

$$P(R \mid Y_{\text{obs}}, Y_{\text{mis}}, \phi) \text{ genuinely depends on } Y_{\text{mis}}.$$

The value that is missing is itself part of the reason it went missing. Here you cannot
recover the truth from the observed data alone — you must supply a model for the
missingness mechanism, and that model rests on assumptions the data cannot verify.

Ignorability. MCAR and MAR are together called ignorable: you may ignore the
mechanism $R$2 and base inference on the observed-data likelihood alone (given a proper
method). MNAR is non-ignorable — the mechanism must be modeled jointly. This single
distinction is the practical payoff of the whole taxonomy.

A general example: sex and body weight in a survey

A questionnaire records sex (fully observed) and body weight (sometimes missing).
The same 30% missing weight can be any of the three mechanisms:

  • MCAR. A printing glitch omitted the weight question from a random subset of forms.
    Respondents never saw it. Missingness is independent of sex and of the true weight.
  • MAR. Women are less likely to report weight than men, but within each sex the
    probability of reporting does not depend on the actual weight. Missingness depends on
    sex — which you observed — so conditioning on sex restores validity.
  • MNAR. Heavier respondents (of either sex) are less likely to report, precisely
    because they weigh more. Missingness depends on the very value that is missing. No
    amount of conditioning on sex fixes this, because the driver (weight) is unobserved for
    exactly the people you need it from.

Notice the same variable, same missing fraction, three completely different correct
analyses. The mechanism — not the percentage — decides.

A pharmacometric teaser

The mechanisms are not academic; they are everywhere in PK/PD data.

  • MAR by design. Neonates are subject to strict limits on blood-draw volume, so they
    have fewer concentration samples than adults. Missingness of a sample depends on
    age — a recorded covariate. This is textbook MAR: a population model that includes
    age (and its maturation function) uses the observed age–sampling relationship and
    remains valid.
  • MNAR from the assay. Concentrations below the lower limit of quantification
    (BLQ/LLOQ)     are reported as "missing" or "<LLOQ." But a value is BLQ because it is
    low     — the missingness depends on the unobserved concentration itself. Naively deleting
    BLQ points biases clearance and terminal-phase estimates upward. This is MNAR, and it
    needs a mechanism model (e.g. the M3 censored-likelihood method) rather than deletion.

You will meet both of these again in later phases. For now, just register that the same
dataset can contain MAR missingness in one column and MNAR censoring in another.

Why the mechanism dictates the method

Mechanism Ignorable? What breaks if you just delete Valid strategy
MCAR Yes Nothing biased; you only lose power Complete-case analysis is unbiased (just inefficient); any proper method fine
MAR Yes Deletion biases estimates unless the missingness predictors are also the analysis conditioning set A method that borrows strength from the observed–missing correlation: multiple imputation (MI) or full-information maximum likelihood (FIML)
MNAR No Deletion and standard MI/ML are biased Model the missingness mechanism explicitly (selection or pattern-mixture models; M3 for BLQ) — relies on untestable assumptions; report sensitivity analyses

The crucial, humbling fact: you cannot test MAR vs MNAR from the observed data alone.
Whether missingness depends on the unseen values is, by definition, about the unseen
values. Distinguishing MAR from MNAR is a matter of subject-matter judgment and design
knowledge, not a statistic you can compute. (You can test MCAR against the alternatives,
e.g. Little's MCAR test — that comparison only involves observed data.)

Worked Example

Scenario. A small clinical dataset records sex, age, weight, and a steady-state
PK exposure (AUC). We reason about weight missingness under each mechanism and predict
the bias in the estimated mean weight.

Assume the truth: mean weight is 75 kg overall; females average 65 kg, males 85 kg; heavier
people have higher exposure. 40% of weights are missing.

Step 1 — MCAR. Missing weights are a random 40%. Observed-weight mean → still ≈ 75 kg.
Bias ≈ 0. Only the standard error grows (fewer rows).

Step 2 — MAR (missingness depends on sex). Suppose females are twice as likely to have
missing weight. The observed sample is now male-enriched, so:

$$\hat\mu_{\text{obs}} \approx \text{(weighted toward 85 kg)} > 75 \text{ kg.}$$

Complete-case analysis is biased upward. But because the driver (sex) is observed, a
model conditioning on sex — or MI using sex — recovers ≈ 75 kg. The observed–missing link
runs through sex, and we have sex.

Step 3 — MNAR (missingness depends on weight itself). Suppose the heaviest 40% simply
did not report. Then the observed weights are the lighter people:

$$\hat\mu_{\text{obs}} \approx \text{(mean of the lightest 60%)} < 75 \text{ kg.}$$

Now conditioning on sex does not help, because within each sex it is still the heavier
members who dropped out, and their weights are gone. Only an explicit mechanism model — with
an assumption about how probability of reporting falls with weight — can correct this, and
that assumption cannot be checked against the data.

Takeaway. Identical 40% missingness produced +0 bias (MCAR), upward bias correctable
with observed covariates (MAR), and downward bias correctable only under untestable
assumptions (MNAR). The code in code/lesson_01.R simulates exactly this and prints the
three biased means next to the truth.

Build it

code/lesson_01.R walks through:

  1. Simulate a complete clinical dataset (set.seed) with age, sex, weight, exposure
  2. Induce MCAR missingness on weight (random 40%)
  3. Induce MAR missingness (probability depends on observed sex)
  4. Induce MNAR missingness (probability depends on weight itself)
  5. Compute the complete-case mean weight under each mechanism and compare to the true mean
  6. Show Little-style intuition: cross-tabulate missingness against sex to reveal the MAR signal
  7. Print a summary table of bias by mechanism

Common Pitfalls

  1. Treating "% missing" as the severity metric. 5% MNAR on your key endpoint can ruin
    an analysis; 50% MCAR on a nuisance variable may barely matter. The mechanism and the
    variable's role dominate the raw percentage.

  2. Reading "MAR" as "the missingness is random." MAR means conditionally random given
    observed data. It is a strong, specific claim, not a shrug.

  3. Believing you can test MAR vs MNAR. You cannot, from the data alone. Anyone who
    claims a p-value proves data are MAR has misunderstood the definition.

  4. Deleting BLQ/LLOQ records "to be clean." That converts an MNAR censoring problem into
    guaranteed bias. Censored values carry information ("it was below X").

  5. Assuming complete-case analysis is always wrong. Under genuine MCAR it is unbiased —
    merely inefficient. The sin of deletion is context-dependent, not universal.

Code

lesson_01.R

# ============================================================================
# Lesson 01 — Missing Data: MCAR / MAR / MNAR in action
# Handling Missing Data in R — Phase 01, Foundations
#
# Goal: build ONE complete clinical dataset, then induce the three missingness
# mechanisms on `weight` and watch what complete-case analysis does to the mean.
# No external files required — everything is simulated with set.seed().
# ============================================================================

set.seed(1976)              # Rubin (1976), for luck

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 1. Simulate a COMPLETE clinical dataset (the data we WISH we had)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
n <- 1000

sex <- factor(sample(c("F", "M"), n, replace = TRUE))          # ~50/50

# Weight depends on sex: females ~65 kg, males ~85 kg (SD 10)
weight <- ifelse(sex == "F",
                 rnorm(n, mean = 65, sd = 10),
                 rnorm(n, mean = 85, sd = 10))

age <- round(rnorm(n, mean = 45, sd = 15))                     # years
age <- pmax(age, 18)                                           # adults only

# Steady-state exposure (AUC) rises with weight (heavier -> higher AUC here)
exposure <- 50 + 2.0 * weight + rnorm(n, sd = 15)

dat <- data.frame(id = 1:n, sex, age, weight, exposure)

true_mean_weight <- mean(dat$weight)
cat(sprintf("TRUE mean weight (complete data): %.2f kg\n\n", true_mean_weight))

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 2. MCAR — missingness independent of everything (a random 40%)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
p_missing <- 0.40
mcar_missing <- rbinom(n, size = 1, prob = p_missing) == 1     # coin flip
w_mcar <- dat$weight
w_mcar[mcar_missing] <- NA

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 3. MAR — missingness depends on OBSERVED sex (females twice as likely missing)
#    (independent of the weight value itself, within sex)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
prob_mar <- ifelse(dat$sex == "F", 0.55, 0.25)                # depends on sex only
mar_missing <- rbinom(n, size = 1, prob = prob_mar) == 1
w_mar <- dat$weight
w_mar[mar_missing] <- NA

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 4. MNAR — missingness depends on the WEIGHT value itself (heavier -> more missing)
#    Logistic in weight: probability of being missing rises with true weight.
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
lin  <- -6 + 0.08 * dat$weight            # centered so overall ~40% missing
prob_mnar <- plogis(lin)                  # 1 / (1 + exp(-lin))
mnar_missing <- rbinom(n, size = 1, prob = prob_mnar) == 1
w_mnar <- dat$weight
w_mnar[mnar_missing] <- NA

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 5. Complete-case (listwise) mean under each mechanism vs the truth
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
cc_mean <- function(x) mean(x, na.rm = TRUE)   # drop NAs, average the rest

results <- data.frame(
  mechanism   = c("Truth", "MCAR", "MAR", "MNAR"),
  n_observed  = c(n, sum(!is.na(w_mcar)), sum(!is.na(w_mar)), sum(!is.na(w_mnar))),
  mean_weight = c(true_mean_weight, cc_mean(w_mcar), cc_mean(w_mar), cc_mean(w_mnar))
)
results$bias <- results$mean_weight - true_mean_weight

cat("Complete-case mean weight by missingness mechanism:\n")
print(results, row.names = FALSE, digits = 4)
cat("\nInterpretation:\n")
cat(" - MCAR  bias ~ 0        : observed cases are a random subsample\n")
cat(" - MAR   bias > 0 (up)   : females (lighter) dropped out -> male-enriched sample\n")
cat(" - MNAR  bias < 0 (down) : the heaviest simply did not report -> lighter survivors\n\n")

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 6. The MAR signal is VISIBLE in observed data: missingness correlates with sex.
#    (This is why MAR is 'ignorable' — the driver, sex, is in hand.)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
cat("MAR: missingness rate of weight by sex (driver is OBSERVED):\n")
mar_by_sex <- tapply(is.na(w_mar), dat$sex, mean)
print(round(mar_by_sex, 3))
cat("Different rates across sex -> MAR is detectable & correctable with sex.\n\n")

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 7. The MNAR signal is INVISIBLE: within the OBSERVED weights you cannot see it,
#    because the informative values (heavy) are exactly the ones now gone.
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
cat("MNAR: mean of OBSERVED weights vs mean of the weights that went MISSING:\n")
cat(sprintf("  observed weights mean : %.2f kg\n", mean(dat$weight[!mnar_missing])))
cat(sprintf("  MISSING  weights mean : %.2f kg  <- unobservable in real life\n",
            mean(dat$weight[mnar_missing])))
cat("The gap is the MNAR bias; no observed variable recovers it.\n")

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# Reproducibility
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
cat("\n----- sessionInfo() -----\n")
print(sessionInfo())

Check

What formal condition distinguishes MAR from MNAR, and why can it not be tested from observed data alone?
  • A. Under MAR, P(R | Y) depends only on Y_obs; under MNAR it also depends on Y_mis. It cannot be tested because the dependence on the missing values is, by definition, about values you never observed.
  • B. Under MAR the missing fraction is below 50%; under MNAR it is above 50%. It cannot be tested because sample sizes are too small.
  • C. Under MAR the data are normally distributed; under MNAR they are skewed. It cannot be tested without a normality assumption.
  • D. MAR and MNAR are identical; the distinction is purely terminological and any test would be circular.

Answer: A

MAR means P(R | Y_obs, Y_mis) = P(R | Y_obs): missingness depends only on observed data. MNAR means the probability genuinely depends on the unobserved value Y_mis. Because that dependence concerns values you never got to see, no statistic computed from the observed data can confirm or refute it — distinguishing MAR from MNAR is a matter of subject-matter judgment, not a testable hypothesis. (MCAR, by contrast, CAN be tested, e.g. Little's test, since it only involves observed quantities.)

In a survey with missing body weight, which scenario is MAR (as opposed to MCAR or MNAR), and does complete-case analysis stay unbiased under it?
  • A. A printing error randomly omitted the question from some forms; complete-case analysis is unbiased.
  • B. Women are less likely to report weight than men, but within each sex reporting is unrelated to the actual weight; complete-case analysis of the raw mean is biased, though sex-conditioned methods recover it.
  • C. Heavier people are less likely to report precisely because they are heavy; complete-case analysis is unbiased.
  • D. Everyone reported their weight; there is no missingness to classify.

Answer: B

Option A is MCAR (independent of everything), and there the raw complete-case mean IS unbiased. Option C is MNAR (depends on the unobserved weight itself). Option B is the MAR case: missingness depends on an OBSERVED variable (sex) but not on the weight value within sex. Under MAR the naive complete-case mean is biased (the sample gets sex-skewed), but because the driver (sex) is observed, a method that conditions on or imputes using sex recovers the correct estimate. That recoverability-from-observed-data is exactly what makes MAR ignorable.

Assay concentrations below the limit of quantification are recorded as missing. Which mechanism is this, is it ignorable, and what happens if you delete those records?
  • A. MCAR; ignorable; deletion is harmless because BLQ values are random.
  • B. MAR; ignorable; deletion is fine as long as you record the sampling time.
  • C. MNAR; non-ignorable; deletion biases estimates (e.g. clearance/terminal phase) because the values are missing precisely because they are low.
  • D. MAR; non-ignorable; deletion improves precision by removing noisy low values.

Answer: C

A value is BLQ because it is below the assay's detection threshold — the missingness depends directly on the unobserved concentration itself. That is MNAR, and MNAR is non-ignorable: you must model the mechanism (e.g. the M3 censored-likelihood method) rather than ignore it. Simply deleting BLQ records discards the systematically lowest values, biasing terminal-phase and clearance estimates. The censored records still carry information — 'this concentration was below X' — which deletion throws away.

MCAR / MAR / MNAR — Decision Tree & Worked-Example Reference

A one-page tool for classifying a missingness mechanism and choosing a valid method.

The Three-Question Decision Tree

                        Does the probability that a value is MISSING
                        depend on ANYTHING in the study?
                                      │
                 ┌────────────────────┴────────────────────┐
                NO                                         YES
                 │                                          │
              ▼ MCAR                    Does it depend on the MISSING value
        (Missing Completely            itself (after conditioning on what
           At Random)                   you observed)?
        Ignorable.                                 │
        Observed = random          ┌───────────────┴───────────────┐
        subsample.                NO                              YES
                                   │                               │
                                ▼ MAR                          ▼ MNAR
                          (Missing At Random)          (Missing Not At Random)
                          Depends only on OBSERVED       Depends on the UNOBSERVED
                          data. Ignorable.               value. NON-ignorable.

Read the tree as three yes/no questions:

  1. Is missingness related to anything? → No ⇒ MCAR.
  2. If yes, is it fully explained by observed variables? → Yes ⇒ MAR.
  3. Does it still depend on the missing value itself? → Yes ⇒ MNAR.

You can test the MCAR branch (e.g. Little's MCAR test). You cannot test the
MAR-vs-MNAR branch from data — it is a subject-matter judgment.

Formal Definitions (one line each)

Mechanism Condition on $P(R \mid Y_{\text{obs}}, Y_{\text{mis}}, \phi)$ Ignorable?
MCAR $= P(R \mid \phi)$ — depends on nothing Yes
MAR $= P(R \mid Y_{\text{obs}}, \phi)$ — observed only Yes
MNAR genuinely depends on $Y_{\text{mis}}$ No

Mechanism Scenario Why Correct approach
MAR Neonates get fewer blood draws (volume limits) Missingness depends on age, a recorded covariate Population model including age/maturation → valid

Method Selection Cheat Line

If mechanism is… …then a valid method is …and this FAILS
MCAR complete-case (unbiased, inefficient), MI, FIML nothing biased; just wasteful
MAR multiple imputation (MI), full-information ML (FIML) complete-case deletion; single mean imputation
MNAR selection / pattern-mixture models, M3 for BLQ deletion and naive MI/ML are biased

The Two Things to Remember

  1. The mechanism, not the percentage, decides. 5% MNAR on your endpoint can be worse
    than 50% MCAR on a nuisance variable.
  2. "Valid" = unbiased and correctly-quantified uncertainty. A single plugged-in
    imputed value fails the second half even when it passes the first.
