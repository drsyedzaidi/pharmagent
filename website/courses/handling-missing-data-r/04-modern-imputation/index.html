Handling Missing Data in R

Phase 04: Modern Imputation — Multiple & Iterative

Phase 03 ended on a cliff-hanger: adding a random residual draw to regression imputation
restores the variance, but a single stochastically imputed dataset still lies about its
standard errors, because it treats the filled-in values as if they were real observations.
This phase resolves that tension. The fix is not a cleverer imputation model — it is to
impute more than once, analyze each completed dataset, and pool the results with a rule
that carries the extra uncertainty of not-knowing directly into the standard errors. That
is multiple imputation (MI) and Rubin's rules. From the principle we build the
workhorse implementation — MICE (Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations, a.k.a. Fully
Conditional Specification) — end to end in R: inspect the missingness pattern, choose
per-variable imputation methods, wire up the predictor matrix, run the Gibbs-like sweeps,
check convergence, analyze with with(), and pool with pool(). Finally we survey the
iterative and machine-learning imputers — the EM algorithm, SoftImpute for
high-dimensional low-rank matrices, and missForest for mixed-type nonlinear data — and
place each on the map of when to reach for it and when it needs an MI wrapper to give
honest inference. Throughout, every method is tested against a known simulated truth,
so you can watch pooled MI recover the true regression coefficient while complete-case and
single-imputation estimates stay biased or over-confident.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the three-step logic of multiple imputation — impute $m>1$ times from the posterior predictive of $Y_{mis}\mid Y_{obs}$, analyze each completed dataset, then pool — and why it beats any single-imputation method
  • State and apply Rubin's rules: the pooled estimate $\bar Q$, within-imputation variance $\bar U$, between-imputation variance $B$, and total variance $T=\bar U+(1+1/m)B$
  • Derive the relative increase in variance $r=(1+1/m)B/\bar U$ and the fraction of missing information $\lambda=(r+2/(df+3))/(r+1)$, and read them as diagnostics
  • Distinguish a proper and congenial imputation model from an improper one, and state the MAR validity condition
  • Run a complete mice workflow: md.pattern, per-variable method, the predictorMatrix, mice(m, maxit), convergence trace plots, densityplot/stripplot diagnostics, with(), and pool()
  • Explain predictive mean matching (PMM) and why it is a safe default numeric method; give practical guidance on $m$, maxit, and predictor selection
  • Describe the EM algorithm (E-step expected sufficient statistics, M-step re-estimation) and why its single completed dataset understates uncertainty
  • Contrast SoftImpute (low-rank SVD + nuclear-norm soft-thresholding) and missForest (random-forest iterative imputation with OOB error) and select the right modern method for a given data shape

Prerequisites

Phase 01 (MCAR/MAR/MNAR taxonomy; the missingness indicator), Phase 02 (missingness
diagnostics; the shadow matrix), and especially Phase 03 (deletion, mean/regression/
stochastic-regression imputation, and why single imputation understates standard errors —
this phase is the direct sequel). Working R with dplyr and ggplot2. Comfort with linear
regression, sample variance/covariance, and the idea of a sampling distribution and standard
error. Install:
install.packages(c("mice", "ggmice", "missForest", "softImpute", "dplyr", "ggplot2", "lattice")).
